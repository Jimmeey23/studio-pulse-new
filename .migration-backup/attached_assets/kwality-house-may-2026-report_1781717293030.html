Financial Insights
Revenue quality is the strongest in 4 monthsMay's net-to-gross ratio is 95.2% — the highest since January. Lower discounting means more revenue is real margin. April's 95.2% net ratio was similar numerically but on 2× gross volume — May's is cleaner.
Membership sales have broken out of their Jan–Mar rangeJan–Mar average was only ₹42,054/month. May and April both delivered ₹3.4L+ — a 7× increase. This is not a blip; it indicates a structural shift in buyer intent toward commitment products.
Drop-in revenue is 26% above the Jan–Mar average₹5.13L vs ₹4.08L average. Drop-in clients (124 transactions) represent the highest conversion opportunity — they're already inside the studio, already paying. A structured post-drop-in offer converts these casual clients.
Promo dependency is at 55.5% of gross — above the baselineJan avg was 45.3%. May's 55.5% means more than half of all revenue flows through promotional pathways. While discount penetration at 15.6% is healthy, the underlying promo architecture is broader than it appears.
Online / System sales are down versus January₹5.59L vs ₹7.68L in January — a 27% drop. This may indicate declining self-serve bookings or a shift toward front-desk / in-person transactions. Worth monitoring to see if it reflects digital journey friction.
Retail is on a sustained growth trajectoryJan ₹67K → Feb ₹49K → Mar ₹85K → May ₹72K. The trend excludes February's dip and shows consistent demand above ₹65K. Retail is an under-leveraged revenue line with strong organic pull.
Money credits are declining — a positive signCredit usage dropped from ₹9,705 (Jan) to ₹2,686 (May). Credits are often issued as compensation for service issues. A declining trend suggests fewer operational grievances being resolved through credit issuance.

Session Insights
Strength Lab is capacity-constrained, not demand-constrained80.7% fill across 60 sessions with a maximum of 7 clients per class. The bottleneck is the small room capacity, not client interest. Adding Strength Lab time slots — particularly Wed 8am, Mon 17:00 and Tue 18:00 — would absorb existing demand immediately.
Studio Mat has the highest revenue per check-in (₹842)The highest revPAX of any regular format. 12.5 avg clients per session at 62.3% fill also means it handles larger groups well. This format is both popular and economically efficient — the clearest case for expansion.
Powercycle is over-indexed at 83 sessions — nearly 3 per dayAt 50.8% fill and 5.1 clients/session against a ~10-seat capacity, Powercycle is being scheduled at 1.6× the demand. Over-scheduling deflates the perceived value and creates trainer fatigue. Cutting 20 sessions (to ~63/month) and concentrating them in high-heatmap slots would push fill toward 65%+.
Studio Barre runs 75 sessions at 38.7% fill — a scheduling imbalanceDespite being the highest revenue format by volume (₹4.71L), it is systematically underfilled. 75 sessions at 1,292 total capacity with only 500 check-ins means 792 empty spots. Pruning 15–20 low-performing time slots would increase fill rate without meaningful revenue loss.
Cardio Barre Express must be discontinued2.3 clients/class across 9 sessions. The 22.2% cancellation rate is also the highest of any format — clients who do book are frequently cancelling. The format has no viable future at Kwality House in its current configuration.
Hosted events are the studio's most efficient fill vehicleAditya's Barre Hosted Class: 18/20 (90%). Harshvi's Birthday Barre: 17/22 (77%). These events consistently fill without requiring discounts. A structured monthly hosted-class calendar — 2–3 per month — would sustainably drive both fill and new lead acquisition.
Late cancellation rate in Cardio Barre Express is 22.2% — an anomalyClients are booking speculatively and cancelling late. This inflates the format's apparent demand while blocking real bookings. The late cancel fee would have its highest impact here.

Root Cause Analysis — Why Are Members Lapsing?
No proactive renewal outreach process existsThe data shows memberships expiring across every week of May with no visible renewal capture. In a functioning retention system, 20–35% of expiring members renew before or within 7 days of expiry. The 0% renewal rate strongly suggests there is no automated or manual pre-expiry renewal reminder in place.
Studio 1 Month Unlimited is a structural trap: 246 lapsed, ₹0 avg LTVThis product has the highest historical churn volume and generates zero long-term value. It is being purchased as a cheap trial entry and abandoned. The product design needs to include a mandated upsell moment: on Day 20 of the month, front desk should initiate every 1-month unlimited holder for a renewal conversation.
Studio 3 Month Unlimited shows ₹335 avg LTV despite a 3-month commitment — a pricing investigation is needed66 lapsed members averaging only ₹335 total spend on a 3-month product suggests either the product has been sold at near-zero cost repeatedly (heavy discounting), or the data is capturing a specific promotional cohort. Cross-reference against discount history.
Annual and 6-Month Unlimited LTV shows ₹0 across 56 historical lapses — a data quality red flag34 Annual + 22 Six-Month = 56 high-commitment members with zero recorded LTV. This is almost certainly a data pipeline issue — these members would have paid thousands of rupees for these products. Reconcile this data against payment records before drawing strategic conclusions.
Proximity is cited by 7 leads as a reason for disengagementWhile these are leads rather than existing members, proximity concerns from the acquisition funnel likely also explain some membership lapses. Members who live or work far from Kemps Corner are at higher churn risk when motivation dips. This is structural and partially beyond studio control, but should inform targeting and scheduling strategies.
The sessionsUsedPct, avgSessionsPerMonth and daysActive columns are empty for all 54 expirationsThese three data fields — which would reveal whether clients were lapsing after heavy or light usage — are missing for every expiring member. This is a data collection failure. Without usage data, it is impossible to distinguish "used-up-and-left" from "disengaged-and-abandoned" — two very different retention problems requiring different interventions.
